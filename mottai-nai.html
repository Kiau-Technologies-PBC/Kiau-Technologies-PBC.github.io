Waste as an issue

Our waste problem is born at the factory, not the curb

Source-first thinking beats end-of-pipe fixes.

We treat waste like an end-user chore, but the real leverage is upstream: material choices, modularity, repairability, and business models that never pretend products are disposable. When those decisions are wrong, we get pollution, stranded value, and brittle supply chains.

Volume

Mountains of hardware churn

Short refresh cycles stack up as toxic e-waste. Designs that ignore second and third lives lock materials in landfills.

Complexity

Invisible risk in the traces

Tightly coupled assemblies are hard to repair or separate. That fragility turns minor faults into full replacements.

Impact

Materials out of place

When inputs cannot return safely to ecosystems, the burden shifts to communities. True stewardship starts before production.

What is mottai-nai?

A four-beat manifesto for respect and regeneration

Mottai-nai began as a gentle reminder to not waste a meal. Wangari Maathai amplified it into a global call to treasure every resource and community that bears the cost of extraction. For us it translates into four recurring questions we ask in every build.

Respect

Who paid for this material in land, health, or labor? Design choices must honor that cost.

Reduce

Can we do the job with fewer atoms or less compute? Efficiency is an act of care.

Reuse

Does this form invite repair, remix, and cascaded lifecycles? Modularity keeps value in circulation.

Regenerate

Will the material return to soil or to a productive loop? Waste should become input, not burden.

Solution (in progress)

Design upstream, so downstream disappears

We are prototyping this right now

We are building practices and tools that catch waste at the sketch stage. Every experiment here is aimed at making reuse the default and disposal the rare exception.

1. Material intelligence

Trace inputs, score their circularity, and swap in regenerative options before procurement locks in.

2. Modularity by default

Architect products for repair, upgrade, and remanufacture. Components live multiple lives across programs.

3. Circular service model

Build take-back and refurbishment into the business model so users never face waste decisions alone.
